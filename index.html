Bringing Zora Neale Hurston's writings to life through archives and technology
By: Kimber Thomas, Ph.D., & anAYA PATEL

About the Project

Zora Neale Hurston was a pioneering writer and anthropologist whose work documented the lives, stories, and cultural expressions of Black communities across the American South and the Caribbean. Her ethnographic approach was revolutionary, capturing not only the voices but the essence and spirit of African American folklore. Through her fieldwork and storytelling, Hurston preserved an authentic record of Black life, language, and tradition that continues to shape literature, history, and cultural studies today.

“Spunk," one of Hurston's early short stories, was first published in 1925 and featured in Alain Locke's The New Negro, a cornerstone anthology of the Harlem Renaissance. The story, set in Eatonville, Florida—the first all-Black incorporated town in the United States and Hurston's hometown—explores themes of masculinity, power, fate, love, and the supernatural. Through “Spunk," Hurston brought to life the unique expressions and complexities of Eatonville's residents, blending folklore with fiction to portray Black life and cultural identity on her own terms. The story demonstrates Hurston's ability to infuse everyday experiences with folkloric depth, creating narratives that resonate with readers while preserving cultural heritage.

In honor of “Spunk's" centennial, Kimber Thomas and Anaya Patel of the Library of Congress's Connecting Communities Digital Initiative (CCDI) produced an animated film adaptation of the short story that reimagines it for contemporary audiences. Drawing from Hurston's archival photographs, field recordings, and manuscripts housed at the Library of Congress, the adaptation combines historical fidelity with digital storytelling, inviting a new generation to step into and explore Hurston's world.

This page provides a behind-the-scenes look at the creative process of bringing “Spunk" to digital life, showing how we used archives and advanced digital tools—including 3D modeling software, audio editors, and game engines—to create historically accurate characters and to construct a detailed 3D representation of early 20th century Eatonville. We also describe how we used Hurston's field recordings from the WPA Florida Folklife Collection at the Library of Congress to build the film's score, adding a soundscape that deepens and enriches the immersive experience.

By combining archival preservation with digital innovation, this project amplifies Hurston's writings and legacy, making her work accessible to new generations and allowing audiences to experience Black culture and folklore as she documented it. Through this reimagining of“Spunk," we invite audiences to explore Hurston's world and to connect with the cultural richness she captured in Eatonville and beyond. The animated film adaptation and 3D interface both redefine how we access and engage with Black history, and this digital storytelling approach highlights the power of archives and technology in illuminating the depth and richness of Black history.

Behind the Scenes: A Look into the Creative Process

Building the Environment

Hurston's archival materials at the Library of Congress were central to our digital reconstruction of early 20th-century Eatonville. Using a game engine, we developed a 3D environment that brings to life the worlds Hurston depicted in "Spunk" and other stories. Key locations were positioned within the virtual town to mirror Eatonville's social structure. Reverend Haynes's Methodist Church, modeled after archival photographs, stands at the community's center, reflecting its role as both a spiritual and cultural hub. Nearby, Joe Clarke's store—an active social space in Hurston's work—was digitally recreated using 3D assets to capture the vibrant community interactions Hurston described. To achieve historical accuracy, we transformed archival photographs into precise digital models, capturing Eatonville's architecture and spatial layout. We then layered in realistic textures and lighting effects to enhance immersion, allowing audiences to experience Eatonville just as Hurston did, making her world tangible and inviting users to explore the spaces and stories she preserved.

modeling the Characters

The characters in “Spunk"—Lena, Spunk, Joe, and Elijah Mosley—were inspired by archival images of Eatonville's residents from the 1930s, drawn from Hurston's own fieldwork photos. The archives provided invaluable insight into the everyday lives of Eatonville's residents during the early twentieth century, allowing us to create characters that feel rooted in the time and place of “Spunk." Paired with Hurston's field recordings, the characters bring Eatonville's community to life, connecting viewers to the everyday people at the heart of Hurston's stories.

CREATING the Film score

The soundtrack to "Spunk" was an important part of the storytelling, adding depth and authenticity to the film. Drawing from Hurston's field recordings in the WPA Florida Folklife Collection at the Library of Congress, AHHA intern Anaya Patel crafted a soundscape that immerses audiences in the rich sounds of Eatonville. By restoring and layering songs, chants, and conversations that Hurston documented, the score not only sets the tone but also grounds the narrative in Hurston's legacy, creating an atmosphere that vividly connects viewers to her world.
